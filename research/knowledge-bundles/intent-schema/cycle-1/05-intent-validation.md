# Research: How can design intent be validated for coherence, completeness, and consistency? Research: (1) Requirements validation techniques in software engineering — how requirements are checked for completeness, consistency, unambiguity, and traceability. (2) Consistency checking in goal models — how GORE frameworks detect contradictions between goals and identify conflict resolution strategies. (3) Design brief review processes — how creative directors and design leads evaluate whether a brief is complete enough to design against. (4) Completeness criteria for UX requirements — what constitutes a 'sufficient' specification of user needs. (5) Conflict detection in multi-stakeholder requirements — how competing priorities from business, users, brand, and technology are surfaced and resolved. (6) Requirements negotiation and prioritization frameworks (MoSCoW, Kano, weighted scoring). (7) How under-specification is detected and handled — when is a requirement too vague to act on, and what makes it actionable? Output: methods for determining whether a stated design intent is coherent (non-contradictory), complete (sufficient for design reasoning), and actionable (specific enough to evaluate against).

## Findings

### Output

Methods for validating a stated design intent focus on three core pillars: coherence, completeness, and actionability. To ensure coherence and non-contradiction, teams should employ structured requirements reviews and goal-model consistency checks (GORE) to identify logical conflicts or circular dependencies. Multi-stakeholder conflicts are resolved through prioritization frameworks like MoSCoW (Must, Should, Could, Won't), the Kano model, or weighted scoring to make trade-offs explicit. Completeness is validated by ensuring the design brief includes critical elements such as project context, success metrics, user personas, and technical constraints. Traceability matrices and the generation of acceptance criteria or test cases are used to verify that every requirement is tied to a goal and is verifiable. Actionability is achieved by replacing vague adverbs with measurable targets and applying heuristics like SMART or INVEST. If a requirement cannot be used to write a test or build a low-fidelity prototype, it is considered under-specified. Practical validation involves a triage checklist to confirm the presence of goals, audience, and constraints, followed by expert reviews to flag ambiguity and ensure that designers or engineers can make decisions without constant clarification.
