# Research: How do design schools and studio-based programs teach design judgment, taste, and critical evaluation without reducing design to a set of rules? Survey research on design pedagogy (1990-2025) including: (1) The Bauhaus and its descendants — how the preliminary course (Vorkurs) built visual sensitivity before technical skill. (2) The studio critique (crit) as a teaching method — its structure, how it transfers reasoning patterns, why it works when lecture-based instruction doesn't. (3) Studio thinking habits — what Harvard Project Zero's 'Studio Thinking' framework identified as the dispositions that design education develops (envision, stretch/explore, reflect, observe, engage/persist). (4) How experienced faculty model expert reasoning during desk crits and pin-ups — making tacit knowledge visible through demonstration rather than rules. (5) Scaffolding strategies that successfully move novices toward expert-like reasoning — what works vs what produces mere compliance. (6) The role of precedent study in design education — how students learn to use examples analytically rather than copying surface features. (7) Evidence on whether 'design taste' can be taught explicitly or must emerge through practice. Output: what design education teaches us about encoding expert reasoning as transferable scaffolds rather than rules.

## Findings

### Output

Design education encodes expert reasoning not as rigid rules, but as transferable scaffolds—flexible supports that guide students toward internalizing expert judgment and taste. This is achieved through several key pedagogical mechanisms:

- **Cultivating Dispositions (Studio Thinking):** Rather than teaching 'correct' answers, programs focus on 'Studio Habits of Mind'—such as envisioning, observing, reflecting, and stretching/exploring. These habits serve as heuristics for judgment that students apply across diverse projects until they become internalized dispositions.
- **Embodied Modeling and Demonstration:** During 'desk crits,' faculty perform reasoning aloud and demonstrate physical moves (sketching, material gestures). This makes the expert's tacit knowledge visible in situ, allowing students to observe and eventually imitate the underlying logic rather than just the surface result.
- **Critique as Shared Inquiry:** The studio critique (crit) functions as a social technology for externalizing reasoning. By using Socratic questioning and probing alternatives, instructors force students to articulate their intent and trade-offs, practicing the language of design judgment in a collaborative environment.
- **Cognitive Apprenticeship and Scaffolding:** Effective instruction uses sequenced tasks and 'fading' supports. Instructors provide prompts and exemplars to drive meaning-making, gradually withdrawing help as the student gains autonomy. This prevents mere compliance and fosters adaptive expertise.
- **Analytical Precedent Study:** Students are taught to 'read' precedents by decomposing the constraints and decisions behind them. This allows for the transfer of principles and tactics to new problems, rather than the superficial copying of forms.
- **Perceptual Foundations (The Bauhaus Model):** Following the Bauhaus Vorkurs tradition, early education prioritizes visual and compositional sensitivity (rhythm, contrast, materiality) before technical specialization. This ensures that later technical decisions are grounded in a developed 'eye' or perceptual fluency.
- **Reflection-in-Action:** By prompting students to reflect during the making process, educators turn episodic experiences into durable procedures for judgment. Taste emerges through this iterative feedback loop, where perceptual differences are linked to specific reasons and criteria.

Ultimately, these scaffolds work because they recreate the epistemic context of expert practice, allowing novices to practice high-level reasoning with temporary support until it becomes an intuitive, internalized skill.
