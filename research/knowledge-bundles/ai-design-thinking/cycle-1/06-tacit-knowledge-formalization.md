# Research: How do situated cognition, tacit knowledge, and embodied cognition theories explain design expertise — and what does this mean for whether design thinking can be formalized or taught to AI? Cover: Polanyi's tacit knowledge applied to design, Schon's reflection-in-action, situated learning theory in design education, whether design intuition is pattern recognition that can be made explicit, and research on externalizing tacit design knowledge (design rationale capture, design intent documentation). Also cover: what design schools have learned about teaching design thinking from scratch — what transfers explicitly vs what only comes through practice. Output: which aspects of design thinking are formalizable vs which may be fundamentally resistant to explicit encoding.

## Findings

### Output

Design expertise is explained through several theoretical lenses that emphasize the non-explicit nature of creative work. Michael Polanyi’s theory of tacit knowledge suggests that designers 'know more than they can tell,' relying on embodied skills and judgment that resist full articulation. Donald Schön’s 'reflection-in-action' describes design as a 'conversation with the situation,' where practitioners frame problems and adjust moves in real-time based on situational feedback. Situated learning and studio pedagogy further suggest that expertise is acquired through 'legitimate peripheral participation' in communities of practice, where novices internalize standards through imitation and critique. Additionally, embodied and distributed cognition theories argue that design reasoning is inseparable from physical interaction with tools and environments.

Research into externalizing this knowledge, such as design rationale capture (IBIS, DRed) and design knowledge networks (DKSN), attempts to document the 'why' behind design decisions (issues, intents, and trade-offs). While these tools can structure rationale fragments into knowledge graphs, they often fail to capture the somatic and socialized nuances of the process. Design schools have found that while domain knowledge, formal methods, and explicit heuristics can be taught through lectures, the 'design eye' and situational framing only emerge through extended practice and apprenticeship.

### Formalizable vs. Resistant Aspects of Design Thinking

**Formalizable (Can be taught to AI):**
- **Domain Facts and Constraints:** Rules, specifications, and material standards.
- **Explicit Methods:** Iterative design cycles, checklists, and formal evaluation metrics.
- **Pattern-Based Expertise:** Recurring solution fragments and statistical patterns learned from large design corpora.
- **Rationale Fragments:** Documented issues, intents, and pros/cons captured through structured tools.

**Resistant to Explicit Encoding:**
- **Somatic/Embodied Know-how:** Sensorimotor skills and the physical 'feel' of materials.
- **Real-time Reflection-in-Action:** The ability to intuitively reframe a problem during the act of creation.
- **Aesthetic and Value-Laden Judgment:** Determining what is 'elegant' or 'humane' within specific, shifting social contexts.
- **Socialized Tacit Knowledge:** Norms and insights that only emerge from long-term participation in a professional community.
