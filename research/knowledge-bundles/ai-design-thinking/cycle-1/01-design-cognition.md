# Research: What is design thinking as a cognitive process — not the IDEO five-step framework, but the actual mental acts expert designers perform while working? Research cognitive science, design studies, and protocol analysis literature (1990-2025) on: what designers actually think about moment-to-moment, how they hold visual hierarchy in working memory, how they resolve competing priorities (aesthetics vs function vs constraint), what it means to have design intuition vs learned rules, and how they know when something is 'wrong' before they can articulate why. Include studies using think-aloud protocols, eye-tracking, or expert-novice comparisons. Output: key cognitive mechanisms, landmark studies, and what distinguishes design reasoning from rule-following.

## Findings

### Output

Design thinking as a cognitive process involves several distinct mental mechanisms that differ significantly from linear rule-following. Expert designers engage in 'co-evolution,' where they iteratively reframe the problem while simultaneously generating solution proposals; the problem and solution representations evolve in tandem rather than sequentially. A central component of this process is the 'dialectics of sketching,' where external drawings act as a cognitive loop. Sketches are not just records of internal thoughts but 'pregnant' displays that allow designers to 'see' new emergent properties and affordances, effectively offloading visuo-spatial working memory and enabling parallel exploration of complex hierarchies.

Designers resolve competing priorities like aesthetics and function through 'satisficing' and opportunistic optimization, seeking acceptable balances rather than single-objective optimization. Expertise in design is characterized by 'design intuition,' which is the rapid, pattern-based recognition of familiar configurations and 'chunks' stored in long-term memory. This often manifests as an affective 'feel' or 'mismatch signal'—a pre-verbal sense that something is 'wrong'—which occurs when a candidate solution violates stored perceptual or functional patterns before the designer can articulate the specific cause. Unlike rule-following, which is serial and deductive, design reasoning is abductive, opportunistic, and heavily reliant on visuo-spatial and embodied cognition.
