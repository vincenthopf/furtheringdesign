# Research: How do expert designers make decisions differently from novice designers? Survey empirical research (1985-2025) on expert-novice differences in: visual design, UI/UX design, graphic design, and architecture. Focus on: how experts frame problems vs novices who jump to solutions, how experts use precedent and analogy, how they chunk visual information, how their iteration and self-critique process differs, and what skills transfer vs what must be domain-specific. Include Nigel Cross, Bryan Lawson, Schon's reflective practitioner, and any cognitive load / pattern recognition research. Output: a structured account of what expertise in design actually consists of.

## Findings

### Output

Expertise in design is characterized by a qualitative shift in cognitive strategies rather than just an accumulation of facts. Empirical research across visual design, UI/UX, and architecture reveals several key differences in how experts make decisions compared to novices:

1. Problem Framing vs. Jumping to Solutions: Experts invest significant effort in 'naming and framing' a problematic situation. While novices often treat a design brief as a fixed goal and move rapidly to proposing solutions, experts treat the situation as ill-defined and engage in a reflective dialogue to reframe the problem. This reduces premature commitment and allows for higher-quality solution spaces.

2. Use of Precedent and Analogy: Experts utilize precedents as structured, abstracted templates focusing on deep functional relations. In contrast, novices often retrieve surface-similar examples, making them more susceptible to 'design fixation'—the tendency to copy visible features rather than underlying logic.

3. Chunking and Visual Cognition: Experienced designers perceive visual information in larger meaningful units (chunks). This pattern recognition allows them to compress complex visuals, freeing up working memory for higher-level reasoning. Eye-tracking studies show experts have fewer, more targeted fixations on semantically rich regions compared to novices.

4. Iteration and Self-Critique: Expert iteration is hypothesis-driven and disciplined. They use external representations like sketches as thinking tools to offload cognition and conduct 'experiments.' Their process involves explicit reflection-in-action, whereas novices often rely on trial-and-error or seek external approval rather than applying internal critical standards.

5. Transferable vs. Domain-Specific Skills: Expertise is a hybrid of transferable cognitive routines (framing strategies, analogical mapping, metacognitive control) and domain-specific knowledge (technical codes, material affordances, and field-specific idioms). While the cognitive routines can be scaffolded across disciplines, domain-specific constraints must be acquired through situated practice.

In summary, design expertise consists of superior problem-reframing habits, a vast repository of abstracted precedents, perceptual chunking that reduces cognitive load, and a disciplined, reflective approach to iteration.
